What Are Letter Sounds?

Letter sounds are the sounds letters can represent in words. When children learn to hear those sounds and connect them to letters, reading starts to make sense.

Letters have names and sounds

A letter has a name. The name of the letter B is “bee.” But that same letter can also represent the /b/ sound at the beginning of words like beach, big, bat, and Beach Cat.

That difference matters. A child may know the alphabet song and still need practice hearing the sounds inside words. Letter names help children identify symbols. Letter sounds help children read.

Simple version: The letter name is what we call the letter. The letter sound is the sound we use when we read a word.

Beginning sounds are a good place to start

For many children, the first sound in a word is the easiest sound to hear. You can say a word slowly and ask, “What sound do you hear at the beginning?”

/b/

beach, big, bat, boat

/m/

moon, map, mat, mug

/s/

sun, sand, sit, shell

You do not need to ask your child to spell the whole word at first. Just noticing one sound is real reading work.

Try not to add an extra “uh”

When adults teach sounds, we often accidentally add a little extra sound. We might say “buh” for /b/ or “muh” for /m/. A tiny bit of that happens naturally, but try to keep the sound short and clean.

For example, with bat, the sounds are closer to:

  • /b/
  • short a
  • /t/

If the sounds become “buh-a-tuh,” blending can get harder. Shorter sounds make it easier for children to push sounds together.

Some letters can make more than one sound

English is not perfectly simple. Some letters can represent more than one sound. The letter A can say short a in cat, long a in cake, and other sounds in other words. The letter C can say hard c in cat and soft c in city.

That does not mean you need to teach every sound at once. In the beginning, it is better to teach one useful sound, practice a few words, and come back to other sounds later.

Parent comfort: You do not have to explain all of English in one sitting. Start with the sound your child needs for today’s words.

Use words your child can picture

Letter sounds are easier to learn when the words feel real. A child can picture a cat, a cup, a bug, a sun, or a shell. That picture helps the sound stick.

That is why Beach Cat lessons use a small set of words and then place those words inside a tiny story. The sound is not floating by itself. It belongs to a word, and the word belongs to a story.

What to say when practicing

Keep your language simple. You might say:

“This is the letter B.”

“B can say /b/.”

“Beach starts with /b/.”

“Big starts with /b/ too.”

Then stop before it becomes a quiz. Children learn best when the routine feels safe and repeatable.

What if my child says the wrong sound?

Stay calm. Instead of saying “No,” try giving the sound again.

“That word is bat. Listen: /b/ /a/ /t/. Let’s try it together.”

A gentle correction keeps the child thinking. A frustrated correction can make the child want to stop.

What parents can do tonight

  • Choose one letter sound.
  • Say the sound clearly.
  • Read three or four words that start with or use that sound.
  • Point to the letter when you hear the sound.
  • Read one tiny story that uses those words.

That is enough. One sound, a few words, one tiny story.
